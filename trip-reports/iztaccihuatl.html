The Adventure

I landed in Mexico City hauling a massive 100-liter expedition duffel stuffed with mountaineering gear. First order of business: hit the ATM for some pesos, then grab a taxi to the bus station.

The chaos inside the Mexico City bus station was something else entirely. I navigated the madness until I found the Autobuses de Oriente (ADO) ticket booth and bought a one-way to Puebla. Making my way to the gate, I quickly realized there was no rhyme or reason to any of it ‚Äî buses pulling up, buses driving away, and the only way you'd know yours was ready was from the driver barging in and yelling the bus number and departure time to a sea of people in seats. Pure chaos.

Thankfully, I caught my bus. As we headed east out of the city, I watched the silhouettes of Izta and Popocat√©petl grow ever-closer through the window. The mountains were calling.

The Crew

My guides ‚Äî Patti, Alo, and Ilgamesh ‚Äî picked me up from the hotel in their truck. Already inside was another American, an ex-Navy man named Frank. Frank and I hit it off immediately, swapping stories the entire ride out to base camp. Once at base camp, Alo took Frank and me on an acclimation hike. Alo held a solid pace, and I was right there with him, but could definitely feel the elevation. Our base camp was at 13,050ft, nearly just as high as the highest peaks in the Rockies. This was going to be an adventure.

Patti cooked us all a big dinner back at camp, and we crashed at 6 PM. Sleep would be brief.

The Midnight Push

We woke at midnight, ate a quick breakfast, and stepped into the darkness. Alo and I moved fast ‚Äî but I felt surprisingly comfortable. Ilgamesh and Frank had started a full 30 minutes before us, but it wasn't long before we were reeling them in. Into the Glacier

We reached the edge of the Ayoloco (Stomach) Glacier and Alo paused. The wind had picked up significantly, and was now absolutely whipping. He scanned for the best route, unsure if conditions were safe enough to continue. We watched as a few other groups descended into the glacier bed ahead of us. That was enough confirmation. Alo and I roped up to each other and followed them down ‚Äî a steep, uneven descent that demanded full attention with every step.

It was still pitch black, and the wind was relentless as we crossed the glacier. Nothing but headlamps, rope tension, and trust between us and the other side. We made it through clean, and from there, it was a straight push to the top.

Summit Before Sunrise

Alo and I reached the summit well before sunrise. The wind at 17,159 feet was relentless ‚Äî the kind that makes you question how long you can stay standing. We didn't linger. A few quick moments to take it in, and we turned back down, watching the first light crest across the horizon in the distance.

The descent rewarded us with something the summit couldn't: a gorgeous sunrise unfolding above the Stomach Glacier. The light spilled across the volcanic landscape in waves of orange and gold, and for a moment, the wind didn't matter.

The Way Out

The hike out was long but good. I got to practice my Spanish with Alo the whole way down, stumbling through conjugations while he patiently corrected me between laughs. The mountain had tested us, but now it was letting us go easy. Our pace didn't let go at all.

Victory

When we rolled back into base camp, the news was waiting for us. Alo and I had set the course record on the Izta climb. The fastest ascent ever publicly recorded on that route.
