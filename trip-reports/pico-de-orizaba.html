The Adventure

Dazed and confused after a record-chasing battle with Izta (read the trip report on that one!), it was time for the kingpin of my Mexico expedition. Though I could surely hear him before I could see him, I stepped outside just in time to watch Ilgamesh arrive at my hotel in his rickety old pickup truck.

The 2.5-hour drive eastward toward Mexico's highest peak was filled with all sorts of chaotic stories, and conversation alternated between English and Spanish at what seemed like just the perfect cadence. As we progressed, I monitored the ever-growing silhouette of what would become one of the most challenging climbs of my life.

Into the Unknown

We stopped for a meal at a family-run hostel in Miguel Hidalgo y Costilla before transferring to a beast of a 4WD off-roader for the ascent to Refugio. I hopped in the lifted Jeep and quickly realized... no seatbelts? Mexico truly plays by their own rules. As we bucked every-which-way up the dusty mountain roads, I laughed to myself. This was happening, I was really doing this.

At the Refugio, we met Ahmed, a young guide who would be our basecamp support, cooking us meals, and serving as our safety net if the summit push didn't go as planned. After he whipped up some delicious rice and beans, we crashed at 6 PM, listening to the whipping wind rattle the frame of the Refugio. I drifted off knowing that in just seven hours, I'd be starting my attempt on North America's tallest volcano.

The Battle Begins

1 AM crept up quickly. Ahmed had us covered, with eggs and hot water Nalgene refills as our last comforts before stepping into the cold darkness of the night. We followed el acueducto past the 3 nests without concern. These were our first indicators of being on-route.

We entered Laberinto (the Labyrinth), where many esteemed mountaineers get lost and turn back. Ilgamesh had me covered. Every step of this mountain was familiar to him, and he moved gracefully as though the entire act was rehearsed. We popped on glacier gear by the Sarcofago (the Sarcophagus), and off we went up the Jamapa Glacier, held together by nothing more than an 8mm rope.

It was here on the glacier, completely exposed to the elements, that Type I fun began gravitating toward Type II. While focusing on the criticality of foot placement in this crevasse minefield, we were barraged by Mother Nature's worst. Sustained 45 mph winds with gusts over 50 mph turned the mountain into a frozen battlefield. But we continued, onward and upward.

My hands were losing the fight. The liner gloves and ski mittens that had carried me through countless previous expeditions were completely inadequate against Pico's fury. Frostbite was creeping in quickly when Ilgamesh showed me a mountaineer's secret: unzip your shell slightly and shove your hands under your armpits. Hold them there, through all of the extreme burning sensations, and you will regain feeling. It was a game-changer that likely saved my fingers.

Dawn at 18,491 Feet

We reached the crater rim just as the first light spread across the horizon. Finding shelter from the relentless wind, we watched the sun rise over a sea of clouds that stretched into infinity below us. At first, it seemed that the worst was over, that we had braved the storm. But the altitude was taking its toll - I felt myself nodding off, fighting an eerie drowsiness that signals the early stages of altitude sickness. It didn't take long for us to pack up our gear and head back onto the glacier.

To our surprise, the descent brought only relief, as the sun emerged and temperatures rose. We navigated past crevasses, large and small, around the glacier - reminders of the technical terrain we'd navigated in darkness. By the time we reached base camp, Pico released us from its grip. We'd finally made peace with the giant.

Victory
