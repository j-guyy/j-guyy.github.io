The Adventure

We were off to a rocky start. I had just gotten back from Asia and was completely jetlagged ‚Äî so jetlagged, in fact, that I slept through my Denver to Seattle flight. By the time I landed, it was 1:30 PM, and Mason, Chris, and Bobby had been waiting at the airport for a while. The rental car was in my name, so they weren't able to progress without me.

By the time we got the car and started heading out of the city, we realized there was no way we were making it to the Paradise ranger station in time to pick up our mountaineering permits. That was the first pivot. It wouldn't be the last.

Pivoting

We had originally planned a Saturday ascent, but absurd snowfall was in the forecast, so we decided to climb on Friday instead. And since we'd missed the ranger station, and the snow was coming the next day, our only option was to go car-to-car ‚Äî the full climb in one push, no stopping at Camp Muir on the way up.

We called Harvard Harry on our way out there. His advice was simple: just listen to what the guides are doing for the guided groups, and follow their lead when in doubt.

Into the Night

We slept at a campground right outside the park from 6 PM to 9 PM, then headed into Mount Rainier National Park and began climbing from Paradise at 11 PM. Almost immediately, the rental mountaineering boots that Bobby, Chris, and Mason had picked up started causing problems. The quality was terrible, and the pain was real.

Bobby and Chris made the call to turn around at Camp Muir. The boot issues were just too much. I understood the call, but it threw off our rope team. Mason and I continued above Muir roped just to each other, being especially careful around crevasses with only two on the line.

The Disappointment Cleaver

We made it across the Cowlitz Glacier, through the Cathedral Gap, over the Ingraham Flats, under the Ingraham Icefall, and up onto the Disappointment Cleaver. The route was not super technical, and we were moving well.

On the Cleaver, we passed some guides coming down. I picked their brains. The news wasn't great: lenticular turbulence. The lenticular cloud sitting on the summit wasn't going anywhere, and inside it, conditions were brutal. Mason and I continued our ascent anyway, passing group after group coming down. Nobody had made it through the cloud to the summit.

The Decision

We broke past the top of the Cleaver and headed back onto the glacier. Massive suncups surrounded us ‚Äî an eerie, beautiful sight. But once inside the lenticular cloud, visibility dropped dramatically and conditions worsened fast.

Then came the real problem. Two weeks before our permit date, the DC route we'd been planning to follow had been wiped out by a massive icefall. The only way past it would be to climb the icefall itself. In these low-visibility conditions, that was too sketchy.

So I listened to the guides, and turned us around. Mason wasn't happy, but conditions only got worse as we descended ‚Äî until we finally broke out of the cloud. We had made the right call.

The Descent

Mason and I made it back down and linked up with Chris and Bobby at the bottom. We crashed at Chris' sister Megan's place for the night ‚Äî a much-needed recovery after the long push.

On our way out of the national park, we stopped for dinner at the same place we'd eaten on the way in: Wildberry Restaurant, an epic Nepalese spot. A fitting bookend to a trip that didn't go as planned, but gave us quite the story.

Unfinished Business

Rainier won this round. But the mountain isn't going anywhere, and neither am I.
